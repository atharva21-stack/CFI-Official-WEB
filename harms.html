Health Risks of Smoking Tobacco
Tobacco use remains the leading cause of preventable death in the United States, killing about one in five people each year. On average, smokers die about 10 years earlier than those who have never smoked. Most people know that smoking can cause cancer. However, it can also cause a variety of other illnesses and damage almost any organ in the body, including the lungs, heart, blood vessels, reproductive organs, mouth, skin, eyes, and bones.
How smoking tobacco affects your cancer risk
In the United States, smoking causes about 20% of all cancers and about 30% of all cancer deaths. Smoking accounts for about 80% of lung cancer cases and about 80% of all lung cancer-related deaths. Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer death in humans in the United States.
How smoking tobacco damages your lungs
Smoking damages the airways and small air sacs in the lungs. This injury begins shortly after someone begins smoking, and lung function continues to deteriorate as long as that person smokes. Still, it can take years before enough problems become apparent to diagnose lung disease.
Why do people who smoke have “smoker’s cough?”
Cigarette smoke contains many chemicals and particles that can irritate the upper respiratory tract and lungs. When a person inhales these substances, the body tries to get rid of them by forming mucus and causing a cough. There are many causes for an early morning cough that is common in smokers. Small hair-like structures in the airways (called cilia) usually help clear the lungs of harmful substances. However, cigarette smoke slows this movement, leaving some of the mucus and particles in the smoke in the lungs and airways. While the person is asleep (and not smoking), some cilia recover and begin to work again. When you wake up, your lungs cough as they try to remove the irritants and mucus that have accumulated from the previous day.
How smoking tobacco affects your heart and blood vessels
Smoking cigarettes damages the heart and blood vessels (cardiovascular system) and increases the risk of heart disease and stroke. moking is one of the leading causes of coronary heart disease (CHD), and the arteries of the heart are unable to supply the heart muscle with sufficient oxygen-rich blood. CHD is the leading cause of heart attack and the leading cause of death in the United States. Smoking causes high blood pressure, reduces the ability to move, and makes it easier for blood to clot. It also lowers blood HDL (good) cholesterol levels. All of these are risk factors for heart attack and stroke.

Stopping smoking for your mental health
We all know that smoking cessation improves physical health. However, it has also been shown to enhance your mental health and well-being: it can help improve mood and reduce stress, anxiety, and depression.
Smoking, anxiety and mood
Most smokers want to quit smoking, but some continue because smoking seems to relieve stress and anxiety. The general belief is that smoking helps to relax. However, smoking actually increases anxiety and tension. Smokers are also more likely to develop depression over time than nonsmokers.
Why it feels like smoking helps us relax
Smoking cigarettes destroys certain chemicals in the brain. If a smoker does not smoke for a while, the thirst for another smoker can be frustrating and anxious. These feelings can be temporarily relieved by lighting a cigarette. Therefore, smokers associate mood improvement with smoking. In fact, it was probably the effect of smoking itself that caused anxiety in the first place. Quitting improves mood and reduces anxiety.
The mental health benefits of quitting smoking
When people quit smoking, studies show that:
Less anxiety, depression and stress
Improves quality of life and positive mood
Dosages of some medications used to treat mental health problems may be reduced
