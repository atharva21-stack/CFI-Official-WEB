Cigarettes Burn Holes In Your Pocket

I began eating Kwai when I was 19 years old and stopped until I was 44 or 45 years old. It was introduced to me by my college friends. My mouth was always full of kwai except during eating and sleeping.

My mother used to scold me, and I always replied with the same answer, "I will quit." I tried many times, but it was very hard for me. I was very addicted to it. So I chewed Duma as an alternative. I knew it was bad and eventually I ended up getting addicted to both.

Despite the failures, I didn’t give up. I kept on trying. I avoided hanging out with friends who eat kwai and duma. I stopped chewing kwai in the morning, but the intensity of the craving hit harder after every meal. There were days I ended up buying and chewing again.

I had a severe toothache one day. I went to see a dentist and the moment I opened my mouth, the first thing he said was, "Stop eating kwai and duma. Your mouth is too dirty. If you continue eating kwai and duma in 5 years from now, you will get mouth cancer". My face was so red, I was so embarrassed. And from that day forward, I vowed to myself, "I will never touch again." It took more than a month to finally quit. With self-motivation and prayers, I got through all of the trigger situations. I changed my mindset. I don’t want people to see my stained teeth. I don’t want to get cancer. I want to look clean because, honestly, now when I see people chewing kwai and duma, they all look dirty.

Now I am so proud that I quit. I will never touch it again. It is all about changing our behaviour and mindset. We all know that it is poisonous, so we should not touch it. It could be cigarrete, biri, kwai, or khaini. We must change our attitude and behaviour toward all of these harmful habits.

It is all about changing our behaviour and mindset.

Kong Dhirbon
